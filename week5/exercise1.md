# ICCS 372 — Week 5 In-class Team Activity 1

## Instructor-Observed Sprint Meeting

# Overview

Last week, your team learned the structure and purpose of a sprint meeting.

Now your team will conduct a real sprint meeting while the instructor observes, listens, and provides guidance when needed.

This activity is not meant to punish mistakes. It is meant to help your team practice a professional coordination habit before the project becomes more complex.

The goal is to make sure each team can:

* review recent progress honestly,
* inspect the board and current issues,
* discuss blockers and unfinished work,
* select realistic sprint work,
* assign ownership clearly,
* update sprint notes and the project board,
* and communicate progress to the client/instructor.

---

# Activity — Instructor-Observed Sprint Meeting

## Learning Objective

By completing this activity, your team should be able to conduct a structured sprint meeting that reviews past work, plans current work, assigns responsibility, and produces visible evidence of project coordination.

## Activity Type

Team in-class activity.

## Time Allocation

Recommended duration:

```text
30–45 minutes per team
```

The instructor may adjust the time depending on class size and team progress.

## Instructor Role

The instructor will attend and observe your sprint meeting.

The instructor may:

* ask clarification questions,
* help redirect the discussion if it becomes unfocused,
* point out missing risks or unclear ownership,
* ask the team to check the backlog or board,
* help the team decide whether unfinished work should be moved, split, paused, or dropped,
* and give short feedback after the meeting.

The instructor is not there to run the meeting for the team.

Your team should still conduct the meeting yourselves.

---

# Before the Meeting

Before the meeting starts, your team should have access to:

* your GitHub Project board,
* current backlog items,
* Sprint 1 issues,
* previous sprint meeting notes, if any,
* Architecture Page v1,
* ADR #1,
* and any current blockers or implementation updates.

Your team should quickly decide who will take the following responsibilities during the meeting:

* Facilitator / Coordinator
* Board Operator
* Scribe / Documentation Owner
* Client Communication Owner (For client email update)
* Timekeeper, if useful

Roles may rotate. One student may hold more than one role if needed.

---

# Meeting Structure

Use the sprint meeting structure introduced in Week 4.

## 1. Opening

Start with a short greeting and human check-in.

Suggested prompt:

```text
How do you feel today?
```

Keep this short, but do not skip it entirely.

## 2. Previous Work Review / Retrospective

Discuss recent work honestly.

Questions to answer:

* What did we complete?
* What did we not complete?
* What blockers or issues appeared?
* How were those issues handled?
* Which unfinished items should move forward?
* Which items should be paused, resized, split, or dropped?

## 3. Current Sprint Planning

Discuss the current Sprint 1 work.

For each selected issue, clarify:

* What is the issue about?
* Why is it important for the Sprint 1 demo?
* What is the priority?
* What is the estimated size?
* Who owns it?
* What are the dependencies or blockers?
* How will the team know it is done?

The board should be updated during or immediately after this discussion.

## 4. Closing

Before ending the meeting, confirm:

* Sprint goal
* Selected sprint issues
* Owners
* Main risks or blockers
* Next immediate actions
* Who will send the client update email

## 5. Client Update Email

After the meeting, one team member should send a short update to the client/instructor.

The update should summarize:

* completed work,
* current sprint focus,
* blockers or risks,
* key decisions,
* and next actions.

---

# Required Output

Your team should produce or update the following:

1. Sprint meeting notes
2. Updated GitHub Project board
3. Client update email
4. Individual contribution logs updated by each member


---

# What Good Looks Like

A good instructor-observed sprint meeting:

* follows the meeting structure without becoming too rigid,
* allows all members to speak,
* uses the board as the shared source of truth,
* discusses real progress and blockers honestly,
* makes practical decisions about current sprint work,
* assigns ownership clearly,
* and ends with clear next actions.

This activity is not graded mainly on how much code is already finished.

It is graded on whether the team can coordinate work professionally and make progress visible.

# Possible questions during the meeting/planning

Students may be asked the following questions:

* What is your Sprint goal?
* Which issue is most important for the demo?
* Which item is currently blocked?
* Who owns this issue?
* Is this issue too large and should it be split?
* Is this item truly necessary this sprint?
* What does “Done” mean for this issue?
* Does the board reflect the actual state of the work?
* Does this decision need an ADR?
* What should the client know after this meeting?

---

# Closing Reminder

A good sprint meeting helps the team avoid hidden work, unclear ownership, and last-minute panic.

By the end of this activity, your team should know what you are building next, who is responsible, what risks exist, and what must be communicated to the client/instructor.
