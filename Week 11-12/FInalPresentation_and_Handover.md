# Activity — Final Project Presentation and Lightweight Handover

## Learning Objective

By completing this assignment, your team should be able to present your final software product professionally, demonstrate that the system works, explain the engineering decisions behind it, and hand over the project clearly enough for the client to understand what was delivered.

## Activity Type

Team assignment and team presentation.

## Time Allocation

Preparation: outside class
Presentation: in class

## Duration

Maximum presentation time:

```
45 minutes per team (Not exceeding)
```

Recommended structure:

```
30-40 minutes presentation and product demo
5–10 minutes Q&A
```

There is no strict slide limit. However, your team must not exceed the time limit.

## Instructions

3 Tasks: Prepare a client-style final project presentation and lightweight handover. And repository cleanup.

You are presenting to the client.

The purpose is to show the final product your team has built, explain how it solves the original problem, demonstrate the working system, and honestly discuss the final scope, limitations, and important engineering decisions. 

Your team must also prepare a lightweight handover file so that the client can understand the final state of the project after the presentation.

## TASK 1: Required Presentation Content

Your presentation should include:

1. Product and Users

   * What is the project?
   * What problem does it solve?
   * Who are the main users?

2. Final Delivered Product

   * What can users actually do in the system now?
   * What is the most important workflow your system supports?
   * What features were completed?
   * What changed from the original plan?

3. Product Demonstration

   * Demonstrate the working application.
   * Show the most important user flow from start to finish.
   * Use realistic sample data.
   * Avoid spending too much time on minor screens or small UI details.
   * Mention unfinished or limited parts honestly.

4. Engineering Evidence

   * Show your final architecture diagram.
   * Explain the main components of the system.
   * Explain the main request/data flow.
   * Mention important ADRs or technical decisions.
   * Explain how you know the system works reasonably well.
   * Briefly mention GitHub, issues, branches, pull requests, or project board evidence that you used. The idea is so that your clients would know you have had solid and structured processes in place. (Briefly mention these, don't get lost in them).

5. Handover Summary

   * What is the current final status of the product?
   * What should the client understand about the product at handover?
   * What are the known issues, limitations, or unfinished parts?
   * What would you improve next if the project continued?
   * Where can the important project artifacts be found?

6. Free-style but professional ending to the presentation of your choice.

   * Have at least a 5-minute Q/A session with the audience.
   * End the presentation in a professional way that leaves the client with confidence in your team and your work.
   * Say thank you and show appreciation to your client.

## TASK 2: Lightweight Handover Requirement
(There's a handover.md template at the end of file. Copy the content and create your own handover.md)

Your team must create or update the following:

1. Updated README.md

   * The README should explain how to set up, run, and inspect the project.

2. HANDOVER.md

   * Use the provided handover template.
   * The handover file should summarize the final product, delivered features, changes from the original plan, known issues, and important artifact links.

3. Existing project artifacts

   * Make sure the important artifacts are available and linked.
   * These may include product brief, team charter, user stories, architecture page, ADRs, backlog or project board, Definition of Done, and presentation slides.

You do not need to create a full formal handover report. Keep the handover practical, clear, and honest.

## TASK 3: Final Repository Cleanup

Your team must do a final cleanup of the repository before submission.

This is a small but important professional step.

1. Remove unused template files

   * Remove old files from the original repository template if they are not actually used in your project.

2. Remove unnecessary files

   * Remove files that are clearly not used and serve no real purpose in the final project.
   * Use your best judgment. Do not remove files that are still part of the project evidence or needed for setup, running, or review.

3. Make sure the repository structure makes sense

   * Organize folders and files in a sensible way.
   * Different teams do not need to have the exact same folder structure.
   * The final repository should still look professional, understandable, and reasonably easy to navigate.

## Presentation Advice

Good presentation behavior:

* Show the working product clearly.
* Explain the product as a solution to a real problem, not just as a list of screens.
* Use slides to support the demo, not replace it.
* Make sure the demo flow is prepared before class.
* Be honest about bugs, missing parts, and limitations.
* Explain engineering decisions, not just technologies.
* Make sure every team member understands the whole project.
* Practice once before presenting.
* Stay within the time limit.
* Speak clearly and professionally.

Weak presentation behavior:

* Showing only slides without demonstrating the product.
* Spending too much time on minor UI details.
* Claiming features are complete when they are not working.
* Listing technologies without explaining their purpose.
* Letting only one person understand or present the whole project.
* Presenting an architecture diagram that does not match the actual final product.
* Going overtime because the team did not prepare the demo flow.
* Hiding known issues instead of explaining them honestly.
