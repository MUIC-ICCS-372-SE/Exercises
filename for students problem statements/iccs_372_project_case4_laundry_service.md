# ICCS 372 — Term Project Case Brief

## Case 4 - FreshFold Bangkok Laundry Pickup and Service Tracking

---

### 1. What this case is
Your team is acting as a small software engineering team engaged by a laundry and garment-care business in Bangkok. The business is not asking for a large logistics or enterprise operations system. They want a practical first version of a digital system that would make service requests and order tracking less confusing.

This document gives you the business context, the customer’s situation, and the problems they are experiencing. It is **not** a finished product brief, a full backlog, or a completed system design. Your team must still analyze the case, define the MVP, decide what is included and excluded, and justify how your solution should work.

---

### 2. Business context
FreshFold Bangkok provides laundry and garment-care services for customers who may use drop-off service, request home pickup of items, or communicate service needs through phone calls and chat. Staff currently record requests, special notes, and order progress manually. The process works, but it depends heavily on staff remembering details correctly and updating information consistently.

The owner feels that this way of working is becoming harder to manage as the number of daily requests increases. Even when staff are experienced, the business still spends too much effort checking what was requested, what was picked up, what is currently in progress, and what is ready.

The owner is not looking for a full logistics platform or a large commercial operations suite. They want a practical first step toward a clearer digital workflow.

---

### 3. Current situation
At the moment, service requests may begin in several ways. A customer might ask what services are available, mention special instructions, request home pickup of items, or say they will drop items off later. Staff then write things down, pass information to each other, and try to keep order progress updated manually.

This creates several opportunities for confusion. Home pickup details may not be recorded clearly. Special service notes may be incomplete or easy to miss. Customers may ask about order status, but staff may need to check several places before answering confidently. Even when the business is functioning, the process can feel more fragile than it should.

---

### 4. Notes from conversations with the customer
The owner said that many of the day-to-day problems are small on their own, but together they create friction. One order may be fine, then another has special notes, another needs home pickup, and another customer asks whether it is already ready. Staff can usually figure things out, but not always quickly and not always from one clear source of truth.

A staff member described common situations like these: “Did the customer ask for home pickup or were they bringing it in themselves?” “Was this express or standard?” “Did they mention a stain or special note somewhere in chat?” “Has this order been washed already, or is it still waiting?”

The owner hinted that a useful MVP does not need to automate every operational detail. Even a simpler system that structures requests, captures important notes, and makes order status more visible would already reduce daily confusion.

---

### 5. Main problems observed
From the case information, your team should consider problems such as:

- service requests arriving through multiple channels
- unclear home pickup and drop-off arrangements
- inconsistent or easy-to-miss special service notes
- lack of one clear place to review request and order status
- slow or uncertain answers about order progress
- limited visibility into current workload and request flow

These are starting points for your analysis. Your team should decide which problems matter most for the first MVP.

---

### 6. What the customer hopes will improve
The business wants a clearer process for receiving service requests, recording customer needs, and tracking order progress. They want customers to be able to understand basic service options and make requests more clearly. They also want staff to review requests, see important notes, and update order status in a more structured way.

The owner is not expecting a polished commercial operations platform. They are looking for an MVP that demonstrates how the business could move away from fragmented manual coordination.

---

### 7. Your task
Your team must turn this case into your own project proposal and delivery plan.

In your product brief and later project documents, your team must decide and justify:

- **Stakeholders and user roles** — who the key stakeholders and user roles are
- **User needs** — what the main user needs are
- **MVP scope** — what the MVP should include
- **Out of scope** — what should be excluded from the MVP
- **Assumptions** — what assumptions your team is making
- **Constraints** — what constraints affect your project
- **Risks** — what risks may affect delivery or product usefulness
- **Success criteria** — what success would look like for the first version
- **Usefulness evidence** — what evidence would show that the MVP is useful enough for this case

Do not simply list every possible feature. Your goal is to define a realistic first version that addresses the most important problems within the project time available.

---

### 8. Technical direction to include in your product brief
Your product brief should include an initial technical direction. Briefly state what type of system your team plans to build and what technologies you expect to use, including front end, back end, database or storage, and demo or hosting approach.

These choices do not need to be final at the start, but they should be realistic for your team and suitable for your proposed MVP. If your team later changes a major technical choice, record the reason in an ADR.

---

### 9. Expected project deliverables
Your team will still be expected to produce the course project deliverables, including:

- a product brief or kick-off document
- a backlog with user stories and acceptance criteria
- a project timeline
- high-level architecture and component diagrams
- ADRs
- a design document
- a working MVP
- handover materials as required by the course

This case brief only gives the starting situation. Your team’s job is to turn it into a clear, justified, and buildable software engineering project.

---

### 10. What a good outcome for this case looks like
A good outcome is not the most feature-rich laundry platform. A good outcome is a well-scoped MVP that addresses the business’s real confusion around requests, notes, and status tracking while remaining coherent and realistically limited.

Your team should aim to show that you understood the client problem, made disciplined scope choices, and built something practical enough to be credible.
