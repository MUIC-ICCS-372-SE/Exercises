# ICCS 372 — Term Project Case Brief

## Case 2 - CarePoint Bangkok Appointment and Queue Management

---

### 1. What this case is
Your team is acting as a small software engineering team engaged by a health and wellness clinic in Bangkok. The clinic is not asking for a hospital-grade platform. They want a practical first version of a digital system that would make appointment handling and front-desk coordination clearer.

This document gives you the business context, the customer’s situation, and the problems they are experiencing. It is **not** a finished product brief, a finished backlog, or a complete solution design. Your team must still analyze the case, define the MVP, decide what is included and excluded, and justify how your solution should work.

---

### 2. Business context
CarePoint Bangkok is a small clinic offering several services that customers often discover through social media, chat, word of mouth, or repeat visits. Most appointments are still handled manually. Customers may contact the clinic by phone, by chat, or by walking in and asking questions at the front desk.

The clinic owner feels that the current way of working is becoming harder to manage. Staff members spend too much time answering routine questions, checking schedules manually, and reconciling what was promised to customers with what is actually available.

The owner is not asking for a full medical records system, insurance platform, or enterprise hospital solution. They want something smaller and more practical that would already make the front desk and appointment flow less confusing.

---

### 3. Current situation
At the moment, appointment requests and service questions come from several directions. One staff member may answer a chat in the morning, another may take a phone call later, and a walk-in customer may ask whether there is still a slot available today. Information is often written down in different places or remembered informally.

The clinic can usually get through the day, but the process depends heavily on staff remembering details correctly. When the clinic gets busier, small mistakes become more visible. A customer might be told a time that is no longer available. Another might arrive and find that the front desk cannot quickly confirm the booking details. Staff may also struggle to see the overall queue for the day without checking several notes or messages.

---

### 4. Notes from conversations with the customer
The owner described the current process as “manageable when it is quiet, but stressful when several customers contact us around the same time.” They said that customers often ask similar questions: what services are available, how long each one takes, whether there is a slot today, and what they need to provide to make a booking.

One staff member mentioned that the clinic sometimes has enough information to know that a customer is interested, but not enough to act confidently. A name may be recorded but not a phone number. A requested service may be mentioned, but the time may not be written down clearly. Sometimes the team knows that “someone is coming this afternoon,” but the exact details are not easy to confirm quickly.

The owner also hinted that the clinic does not necessarily need the software to model every real-world detail on day one. They would already benefit from something that makes appointments, basic availability, and today’s queue easier to see and update.

---

### 5. Main problems observed
From the case information, your team should consider problems such as:

- appointment requests handled across multiple channels
- inconsistently recorded booking information
- unclear or overlapping time slots
- lack of a shared view of bookings and daily workload
- incomplete or hard-to-find customer details
- limited visibility into current booking activity

These are starting points for your analysis. Your team should decide which problems matter most for the first MVP.

---

### 6. What the customer hopes will improve
The clinic wants a clearer and more consistent booking flow. They want customers to have a better way to understand available services and request appointments without so much back-and-forth. They want staff to be able to review requests, update booking status, and keep a simple view of the day’s activity.

The owner is not expecting a perfect or final commercial product. They are looking for a practical MVP that shows how the clinic could move from fragmented manual coordination toward a more reliable digital process.

---

### 7. Your task
Your team must turn this case into your own project proposal and delivery plan.

In your product brief and later project documents, your team must decide and justify:

- **Stakeholders and user roles** — who the key stakeholders and user roles are
- **User needs** — what the main user needs are
- **MVP scope** — what the MVP should include
- **Out of scope** — what should be excluded from the MVP
- **Assumptions** — what assumptions your team is making
- **Constraints** — what constraints affect your project
- **Risks** — what risks may affect delivery or product usefulness
- **Success criteria** — what success would look like for the first version
- **Usefulness evidence** — what evidence would show that the MVP is useful enough for this case

Do not simply list every possible feature. Your goal is to define a realistic first version that addresses the most important problems within the project time available.

---

### 8. Technical direction to include in your product brief
Your product brief should include an initial technical direction. Briefly state what type of system your team plans to build and what technologies you expect to use, including front end, back end, database or storage, and demo or hosting approach.

These choices do not need to be final at the start, but they should be realistic for your team and suitable for your proposed MVP. If your team later changes a major technical choice, record the reason in an ADR.

---

### 9. Expected project deliverables
Your team will still be expected to produce the course project deliverables, including:

- a product brief or kick-off document
- a backlog with user stories and acceptance criteria
- a project timeline
- high-level architecture and component diagrams
- ADRs
- a design document
- a working MVP
- handover materials as required by the course

This case brief only gives the starting situation. Your team’s job is to turn it into a clear, justified, and buildable software engineering project.

---

### 10. What a good outcome for this case looks like
A good outcome is not the largest clinic system. A good outcome is a believable MVP with a clear core workflow, sensible scope boundaries, and reasoning that matches the clinic’s real problems.

Your team should aim to show that you understood the client problem, made disciplined choices, and built a solution that is coherent, usable, and honestly limited where appropriate.
